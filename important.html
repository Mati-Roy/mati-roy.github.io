Cryonics

My favorite introduction to cryonics: Why cryonics makes sense? | Wait buy why.

Objections I think are reasonable:

  • (epistemic) If you live somewhere only Alcor can get to you, and you're unlikely to die, and are not doing great financially, then the membership fee might be better invested. Although I would still recommend getting a whole-life insurance for cryonics purposes, and having a clear plan for when you'll sign up.
    *Technically: If you think you're much more likely to die in a cryopreservable way in the future than now, which might not be the case if you have short AGI timelines.
  • (value and epistemic) You're dedicating 100% of your life towards AI alignment.
  • (value) You care a lot more about avoiding a potential bad futures by dying (Not to the point of dying right away, but to the point of not trusting anyone to cremate you when it seems like the world might be heading in a bad direction.)
  • (value) You don't care much about living more than 70-90 years old even in a healthy body in the best possible world you can imagine. → That one seems very suspicious to me, but if true, then makes sense.

Objections I think are problematic:

  • You're sufficiently confident cryonics doesn't preserve the mind → see Engram Preservation: Early Work Towards Mind Uploading | Robert McIntyre
  • You're sufficiently confident that you would value living longer a lot yet value very little having your cryopreserved mind uploaded → see Killed by Bad Philosophy

Lifelogging as life extension

I still haven't published my main pitch of the idea, but here's a short summray.

  • Almost everything everyone knows about you can be recorded.
  • A superintelligent AI with lifelog access could recreate a mind that passes the "identity turing test", that is, a mind that's indistinguishable from you from the perspective of the rest of the world.
  • If we combine that information with your DNA and knowledge of the human brains, we could create a (conscious) human mind passing the identity turing test..
  • Death is a spectrum rather than being binary. Even when you forget some information and your personality changes somewhat, a large part of you is still alive.
  • Even just natural life might be responsible for constant microdeaths which lifelogs and superintelligent AI could be able to help fix in the future.
  • Lifelogging is pretty cheap, so even if you think this argument is unlikely true, it might still be worth it.
  • Lifelogging seems (currently) easily reversable, so you can download an audio recording app right now and press record while you think more about it.
